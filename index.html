Welcome to Miami!

A city rich with diversity, sunshine, all night parties, and beautiful coastline. While visitors of all ages come for the nightlife or a beach getaway, over 6 million people call this metropolitan area home.

Miami is a city particularly vulnerable to sea level rise due to its 17 miles of beach access and low lying topography. Most of the state of Florida sits on a bed of porous limestone, making most traditional climate change relief methodologies ineffective. Let’s take a look at the varying sea levels and climate risks Miami has endured throughout the 2000s, and will continue to endure for decades to come. This visualization is based on both my imagination and scientific fact.

Here we are at the turn of the century. The year 2000 is used as a baseline for measuring and predicting the sea level in Miami. Looking forward, sea levels would rise at an accelerated rate, and climate risks would become detrimental.

Year: 2000

Sea Level: 0.0 in

Let’s jump forward a decade to 2010. That year, Miami experienced further acceleration of rising sea levels and other climate risks, including warmer winters, and an increase in king tides (highest annual tides, causing significant flooding in the streets). Measurements that year showed that the sea level had increased by 2.28 inches. This was only the beginning.

Year: 2010

Sea Level: 2.28 in

In 2020, the First Street Foundation deemed 98% of properties in Miami Beach to be at risk of flooding due to sea level rise. Sea levels continued to rise at an accelerating rate, and king tides and storm surge worsened. Data showed that the sea level had now increased by 4.68 inches, which is more than double the levels in 2010.

Year: 2020

Sea Level: 4.68 in

Let’s move on to the future, where things are looking even more grim. By 2030, scientists have predicted 6-10 inches of sea level rise. We will see continuous flooding, increased storm surge, and saltwater contaminating the city's clean water supply.

Year: 2030

Sea Level: 10 in

By 2040, scientists have predicted sea levels will be 10-17 inches higher than the 2000 mean sea level. Temperatures will continue increasing at an accelerated rate, hurricane relief systems will fail, and homes and infrastructure will be destroyed.

Year: 2040

Sea Level: 17 in

Moving two decades later to 2060, it is predicted that the city of Miami will be 60% underwater, leaving less than half of the entire city habitable. Hurricanes will intensify and temperatures will continue to rise, making Miami a very difficult place to live. At this point, the sea level will be about 2 feet above the baseline 2000 level.

Year: 2060

Sea Level: 24 in

By 2070, scientists have predicted that the city will be between 21 and 54 inches above the 2000 mean sea level. The city will experience daily flooding, causing infrastructure such as roads and the airport to be underwater, and drinking water will be scarce.

Year: 2070

Sea Level: 54 in

And by 2100, the city will have been devastated by up to 6 feet of sea level rise. Most of Miami Beach and the greater Miami area will be underwater, and millions will be displaced. Temperatures will be too hot for trees and wildlife to thrive.

Year: 2100

Sea Level: 72 in

Finally by 2120, the sea levels will be 40-136 inches above the 2000 mean sea level, leaving Miami uninhabitable.

Year: 2120

Sea Level: 136 in
